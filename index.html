Learning Task-Centric World Models from Visual Foundations

A task-centric latent world model that turns frozen foundation embeddings into compact, planning-relevant dynamics for reward-free offline visual planning.

Abstract

World models enable agents to predict the evolution of dynamics under control signals. However, their latents are often either learned from pixels with limited semantic structure or inherited directly from frozen visual foundation models with excessive task-irrelevant detail, yielding state spaces that are poorly matched to downstream planning. We target reward-free offline visual planning, where the model is trained from fixed trajectories and later plans to a goal observation without reward supervision or online interaction.

We propose TC-WM, a framework for turning foundation-model embeddings into compact, task-sufficient world representations. The key design is to treat the pretrained embedding space as a semantic scaffold rather than as the final state space: TC-WM linearly projects high-dimensional visual embeddings into a compact latent, aligns a designated subspace with the agent's physical state via contrastive learning, and reconstructs embeddings to preserve useful visual structure. This combines the generality of foundation features with the controllability of task-centric dynamics. Theoretically, we show that TC-WM suffices to identify the task-centric latent factors up to an invertible transformation. Empirically, TC-WM enables zero-shot planning across diverse environments (e.g., Robomimic and D4RL), achieving better world modeling quality and more precise control compared to state-of-the-art world model approaches.

Overview

World-model research has crystallised into three architectural branches that differ in the level at which dynamics are predicted. Generative models predict future observations directly in pixel space — rich but heavy. Latent models compress the scene into a learned state space and predict dynamics within it. Embedding models (e.g., DINO-WM, V-JEPA) skip representation learning by rolling out directly in the frozen feature space of a pre-trained foundation model.

Foundation embeddings are useful not because they are ideal states, but because they are a strong semantic coordinate system from which states can be extracted. They are optimised for broad semantics — textures, lighting, background — not for control. When used directly as a world-model latent, the model spends capacity on irrelevant variation. The burden is acute under high-dimensional action spaces: Maze uses 2-D actions, but Robomimic requires a 7-DoF arm with 43-D proprioceptive state, where every irrelevant latent dimension is wasted planning capacity.

TC-WM treats the foundation embedding as a reusable visual scaffold and learns compact latent dynamics above it. A designated subspace aligns with physical proprioception via contrastive learning; the complementary subspace stays anchored to the pretrained embedding via reconstruction, preserving useful visual structure while filtering nuisance variation. This decomposition yields an identifiability guarantee for the task-relevant latent and consistent gains in both world-model quality and offline planning.

Method

TC-WM learns a compact rollout state zt on top of frozen visual embeddings, using proprioception to identify the dimensions needed for physical dynamics. The pipeline has six components:

  • Joint Embedding — concatenate a frozen visual feature (e.g., DINOv2) with a trainable proprioception embedding to form the input xt.
  • Latent Encoder — a linear projection q(zt | xt) compresses xt into a compact latent zt.
  • TC Alignment — factorise zt = [zst, zct]; align zst with proprioception via an InfoNCE contrastive loss, with an ℓ1 penalty that automatically selects the task-centric block by sparsity.
  • Latent Dynamics — a ViT transition predicts the next latent &zcirc;t+1 from history.
  • TC Dynamics — a head predicts next-step proprioception directly from the latent history, encouraging task-centric temporal structure.
  • Embedding Decoder — a linear decoder reconstructs xt from zt, anchoring the compact latent to the foundation-embedding manifold and preventing collapse.

The full objective combines latent and proprioceptive dynamics terms with the alignment and reconstruction losses:

Lall = Lzdyn + Lsdyn + λalign Lalign + λrec Lrec.

Architecture. TC-WM builds a world model by extracting a compact, task-centric latent from frozen visual embeddings using proprioception as side information. Latent dynamics and proprioception prediction are trained jointly with alignment and reconstruction objectives.

Theoretical Guarantee

Under standard contextual-dynamics and latent–observed variability assumptions, plus the partial-alignment objective above, the learned latent zt is block-wise identifiable and the task-centric block zst is recovered up to an affine map:

&zcirc;t = hz(zt)  (invertible, C1)      zst = A &zcirc;st + b.

Intuitively, the observable joint distribution over neighbouring embeddings determines an operator family that is a similarity transform of a diagonal operator indexed by p(xt | zt). Injective context operators make this diagonalisation unique up to a smooth reparameterisation, and the alignment loss actively produces the latent–observed diversity that traditionally has to be assumed. Thus the linear projection plus contrastive alignment in TC-WM can recover a compact, physically-meaningful latent — not merely a predictively-accurate one.

Environments

We evaluate on nine offline visual-control environments spanning three task families: navigation (Maze, Wall), manipulation (Push-T and Robomimic's Lift, Can, Square), and locomotion from DeepMind Control Suite (Reacher, Cheetah, Hopper). Action and proprioception dimensions vary from 2-D to 7-DoF / 43-D, stressing the importance of a task-centric latent under high-dimensional control.

Results

We compare against four representative world-model baselines: TD-MPC2 (compact latent dynamics), DreamerV3 (recurrent generative latent), MuZero (task-oriented latent dynamics, reproduced offline), and DINO-WM (the closest embedding-space baseline). All methods see the same offline trajectories of images, actions, and proprioception.

World-model prediction

TC-WM achieves the lowest latent-prediction error on nearly all tasks — especially in manipulation — while remaining competitive in image-space reconstruction. Compressing frozen visual embeddings preserves key structure and yields a more stable state space for rollout.

World-model prediction accuracy. Prediction Reconstruction Loss (PR) and Latent State Prediction Loss (LP); lower is better, over 300k training steps.

Open-loop rollouts on Robomimic. Lift (left half) and Can (right half). TC-WM better preserves contact events, gripper motion, and object placement over long horizons.

Planning performance

We use Cross-Entropy Method (CEM) planning for low-dimensional control and Latent Diffusion Planning (LDP) for high-dimensional manipulation. TC-WM matches the strongest baseline on saturated CEM tasks and is the only method that surpasses DINO-WM on every LDP task, where high-dimensional action spaces make task-centric latent dynamics critical.

Planning performance. (Top) CEM on Maze, Wall, Push-T (success rate) and Cheetah, Hopper (episode return). (Bottom) LDP on Lift, Can, Square, where TC-WM's task-centric latents matter most.

Robustness to unseen perturbations

Under Gaussian noise (σ=0.1) and per-channel color jitter, TC-WM recovers manipulator and object pose under both perturbations; its relative SSIM drop is consistently smaller than DINO-WM's (e.g., −2.5% vs. −4.8% under noise on Lift).

Unseen visual perturbations. Left: Gaussian noise on Square and Can. Right: color jitter on Can and Lift.

Analysis

Linear probing

We probe each method's latent by training a linear regressor to ground-truth full states (proprioception, object pose, goal indicators). TC-WM more accurately recovers these task-centric variables, indicating that its latent is not merely predictive but physically grounded.

Linear probing analysis. Stronger linear recovery of physical state from TC-WM's latent compared to embedding-space baselines.

Architecture and module ablations

Random projection performs poorly — dimensionality reduction alone is not enough. DINOv2 and DINOv3 give the strongest foundation features; Cosmos is weaker in this setting. Among projection heads, the linear map gives the best balance between planning success and embedding reconstruction, consistent with the affine-identifiability prediction from the theory.

Architecture/module ablations on Robomimic. (a) Visual-encoder choice. (b) Projection-head choice. Success rate (left) and SSIM (right).

Loss components

Removing embedding reconstruction causes the largest collapse — it is what anchors the compact latent to the foundation manifold. Removing proprioceptive supervision preserves SSIM but lowers success rate: alignment shapes the latent for control rather than visual fidelity. The zs/zc split dimension has only a modest effect.

Loss-component ablation on Lift. Lrec=0 collapses both planning and visual fidelity; proprioceptive supervision is essential for planning; latent split dimension has modest effect.
