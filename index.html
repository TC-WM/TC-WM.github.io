Learning Task-Centric World Models from Visual Foundations

A task-centric latent world model that distills high-dimensional visual foundation embeddings into compact, planning-relevant dynamics.

Minghao Fu1, Fan Feng1, Nicklas Hansen1, Biwei Huang1
1University of California, San Diego
arXiv (Coming Soon) Code Dataset (Coming Soon)

Abstract

World models enable agents to predict the evolution of dynamics under control signals. However, semantic representations in world models are often overly rich or misaligned with task objectives, which can introduce unnecessary complexity, degrade physical fidelity, and reduce planning efficiency. We propose a task-centric world modeling framework that transforms high-dimensional visual foundation embeddings into a compact latent space that preserves task-relevant information while discarding redundant factors. The resulting representation is partially aligned with critical physical properties and remains semantically faithful for accurate prediction and planning. Empirically, our method enables zero-shot planning across diverse environments (for example, Robomimic and D4RL), achieving stronger world-model quality and more precise control compared with prior approaches.

Overview / Motivation

Modern world models increasingly reuse frozen visual foundation embeddings as latent states. While this improves transfer, the embeddings are optimized for broad semantics, not necessarily for control-relevant dynamics. As a result, latent spaces can retain texture and appearance details that are unnecessary for decision-making, reducing planning efficiency and interpretability.

TC-WM addresses this gap by learning a compact task-centric latent space on top of foundation embeddings. The model preserves predictive information needed for future task dynamics while discarding redundant semantics, enabling more stable and precise zero-shot planning across diverse offline benchmarks.

TC-WM architecture: frozen visual embeddings are distilled into a compact latent world model with partial task alignment and dynamics prediction.

Contributions

  • A task-centric world modeling framework that extracts compact planning-relevant latents from high-dimensional visual foundation features.
  • Partial alignment between latent subspace and task-critical physical variables (for example proprioception), balancing semantic fidelity and controllability.
  • Joint latent dynamics and task dynamics objectives that improve predictive sufficiency for downstream goal-conditioned planning.
  • Consistent gains in world-model quality and planning performance across 9 environments spanning Robomimic, D4RL, and DMControl offline settings.

Method

TC-WM builds a world model in three stages: encode observations with a frozen visual backbone, project embeddings to a compact latent space, and learn temporal dynamics for both latent transitions and task-centric variables. Instead of predicting directly in the original visual embedding space, TC-WM reshapes representation structure for physical consistency and planning.

The key design is partial alignment: only a designated latent subspace is aligned with observed task-relevant signals, while the remaining latent factors preserve complementary semantics. This decomposition improves controllability without collapsing representation capacity.

Results

Quantitatively, TC-WM achieves stronger latent prediction accuracy and competitive image-space rollout quality versus prior world-model baselines. The performance advantage is especially clear in long-horizon planning with high-dimensional action spaces.

Planning performance: TC-WM improves success rates in both CEM (navigation/low-dimensional action) and LDP (manipulation/high-dimensional action) settings.

World-model prediction metrics over training: TC-WM maintains lower latent prediction error across most evaluated tasks.

Lift rollout: more stable long-horizon manipulation and grasping behavior.

Can rollout: improved object placement consistency near goal states.

Square rollout: stronger temporal stability in contact-rich assembly dynamics.

Representation analysis: linear probing indicates stronger encoding of task-relevant factors in TC-WM latents.
